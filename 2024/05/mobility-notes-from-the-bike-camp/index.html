Mobility notes from the bike camp

I was at a mountainbike camp in Finale Ligure recently. I don’t count the mountain biking we did there as mobility, let’s look at how I got there, what worked and what didn’t.

mode of transportation

I came by train and brought my bike along.

Spatial constraint

A corner that I cut: I didn’t check the small print of the train companies involved about the exact measures of bicycles that are allowed on the trains. I expect issues in width and length.

Outcome: It worked well, the train crews didn’t complain and the bike racks were never booked out (means: wasn’t tight for space).

Backup solution width: I had the hex key ready to turn the handle bar by 90 degree. The width of the handle bar on the mountainbike just unpractically wide and a challenge on public transport. I’m pretty certain it is too wide for the actual regulations, and some small prints certainly say one has to turn the handle bar.

Length: My bike’s whell base is too long for Spanish trains. In the sense that the wheel base (middle of front wheel to middle or rear wheel) already exceeds the specs. In practical terms, I know that on the Swiss FV Dosto The bike fits just about if I hang it on the rear wheel and compress the fork (practically requires deflating the fork). The situation looks similar on the Giruno. I had a fork pump with me, but stuck deep inside the luggage. I had used that option in previous years, where the bike leg to/from the station was much longer, such that I could adapt to going on tarmac with all my luggage (instead of just a day-pack).

NB: the space concerns probably don’t affect city or touring bikes as much, so for combined bike+train mobility (when we don’t consider the bike as sport equipment) you’ll probably be fine.

Booking reservation

What you need: long distance trains (usually) require reservations for bicycles. (Actually a new thing in Switzerland, otoh Italy requires seat reservations also w/o bike).

What worked: Compared to a few years ago, booking the international trip online in self service worked. SBB doesn’t offer it, but trenitalia does.

What’s annoying: With the reservation you’re locked onto a connection. Means stress in case you don’t make a connection. Means you can only pick connections that the booking system offers you. “More layover at X and less at Y” is hard / impossible to pass. Also means for the return trip you can’t leave “as you feel like”. Also: bike reservations can’t be cancelled. So even if you’re happy with just reserving additionally on a different train than originally planned, you can’t vacate your spot for somebody else. Have that on your conscience. (TBF: I understand that it’s not good if people reserve until last minute “just in case” and then cancel their reservation, for nobody to realistically pick it up. But like 2 CHF for reserving, 1 CHF in return for liberating until x hours in advance would still leave an incentive to cancel a reservation and disincentivise blocking off all spots).

What’s just designed to fail: Neither domestic trips on SBB nor the international trips with trenitalia (probably also not the Italian domestic trips) can reserve bike spot and seat in the same coach. For my trip, I even had my seat and the bike spot in different sections of the train, such that I couldn’t go towards the bike to prepare disembarking.

What I actually did: I didn’t care about where my actual seat was, just sat near the bike, all went fine. I did however ask the Italian train crew before boarding if that was okay with them. The only cared about me having a valid ticket for that train. I guess it’s also a matter of practicality. Once reservations are mandatory, the train can’t be overbooked and there’s a seat for everybody.

Milano centrale

The station is a bit of a mess, in my opinion. Platforms are announced rather late, sometimes long after the train is actually ready at the platform. The underpassage - while shortening many trips and recently renovated - is deserted. Nobody waits on a platform and the cross platform at the head end is over crowded with people waiting for their platforms and has no waiting area or capacity to hold all the people.

Regional trains

The combination of regional trains + long distance trains is also a bit of a sore spot. There’s no reservation for regional trains, which is nice for flexibility and less hazzle, but means it might be too full to get in (technically the number of bike spots is limitted and they can refuse to transport you, especially for evacuation safety) you might not manage to get to the departure of your reserved long distance train. (Same issue as with postbus+IC combinations in Switzerland, except postbus now also introduces reservations). Online regional tickets in italy are not fixed to specific trains anymore (they gained the flexibility of the physical ticket), but that means one has to “stamp” them, just like physical tickets, which is best done with the trenitalia app (website should also work). I’m not a great fan of installing yet another app for that, but what I like is that it’s at least one app for all of Italy and not a federal mess of per-regional operator apps.

layovers

Compared to the Swiss train network, the Italian is frustrating in places. The regional train leg I had, had a gap between trains of 2h, but then 2 trains within 10 minutes. And on top of that, schedules of different lines don’t seem aligned to make good connections. I had one 1h59min layover to get onto a regional train that ran only every 2h. To be fair, there were works ongoing to increase the capacity of the line (thanks EU, btw), so that may have been exceptionally sparse intervals in low season. And, due to reservations and prejudice of delays, I anyway wouldn’t have picked 10min layover connections.

Gotthard base tunnel

I travelled anti-cyclic on the long weekends. Which was nice not to have everything booked out and crowded. But also means that (due to a recent accident) passenger trains only go on holidays in the main travel direction through the tunnel, and I went (against the main traffic) for a 1h detour through the summit tunnel.
